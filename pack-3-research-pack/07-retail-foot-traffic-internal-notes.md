RE: retail foot traffic trend — potential flagship expansion

The Q1 retail audit is complete. Four stores (New York, LA, Nashville, Austin).

HEADLINE
All four stores are up in foot traffic YoY. NY and LA have the most striking growth (+38% and +41% respectively). Nashville is steady (+12%). Austin is slightly down (-6%) and I'll note some context on that below.

What matters more than foot traffic: conversion-to-transaction rose across all four stores. We are doing better at turning browsers into buyers. That's a real result.

YEAR-OVER-YEAR FOOT TRAFFIC BY STORE

- New York (Williamsburg): 38% YoY
- Los Angeles (Venice): 41% YoY
- Nashville (12 South): 12% YoY
- Austin (South Congress): -6% YoY

CONVERSION TO TRANSACTION

- New York: 16% → 22%
- Los Angeles: 14% → 20%
- Nashville: 19% → 24%
- Austin: 12% → 17%

Average order value in-store:

- New York: $184 → $208
- Los Angeles: $168 → $195
- Nashville: $152 → $171
- Austin: $143 → $156

AUSTIN CONTEXT

Foot traffic decline in Austin is almost entirely explained by the construction on South Congress from January through mid-March. The sidewalk was torn up directly in front of the store. Conversion-to-transaction went up — people who did walk in were more intent. I don't think this is a Haven & Hearth demand issue, I think this is a literal-sidewalk issue.

IMPLICATIONS FOR FLAGSHIP EXPANSION

The in-store numbers say: more stores = good. The conversion rates in-store (17-24%) are higher than our online conversion rate (3.8%). The AOV in-store is higher than online AOV ($184 vs. $112).

My recommendation is to push for two new flagships in 2027: Denver and Boston. Both have strong online customer concentration and no direct competitor retail presence today. Denver is particularly under-penetrated — we've had multiple customer interviews in Q1 that specifically named Denver as a retail gap.

Parachute and Boll & Branch are both expanding retail in 2026. If we wait until 2028, we're competing for leases in the exact same neighborhoods we want.

COUNTER-ARGUMENT

Retail is capital-intensive and slow. If our subscription box (Home Ritual) is growing 80% YoY and retail expansion would require us to pull marketing dollars from that channel, we should think twice. There's a version of the next 24 months where we take the same capital and push Home Ritual into the $15M run rate, and the unit economics are frankly better.

I'll draft two memos for the leadership offsite: one arguing the retail case and one arguing the subscription case. I think the honest answer is we can do both at half speed, and that's probably what we should do.
